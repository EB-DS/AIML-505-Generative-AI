1. Introduction¶

Generative Artificial Intelligence (Generative AI) represents one of the most significant technological advancements in contemporary computing, fundamentally transforming how organizations create, analyze, and interact with information. Unlike traditional artificial intelligence systems that primarily focus on prediction or classification, generative AI models are capable of producing novel content—including text, images, audio, computer code, and multimodal outputs—by learning complex statistical patterns from large-scale datasets (Goodfellow et al., 2016). Recent advances in transformer-based architectures and Large Language Models (LLMs) have further expanded these capabilities, enabling machines to perform sophisticated reasoning, language understanding, knowledge synthesis, and decision support across diverse organizational contexts (Vaswani et al., 2017; Brown et al., 2020).

The rapid adoption of LLMs is reshaping business strategy, knowledge management, and organizational decision-making. Modern enterprises increasingly employ generative AI to automate routine knowledge work, generate executive reports, support customer engagement, assist software development, and enhance strategic planning. Consequently, effective leadership in the age of artificial intelligence requires more than technical awareness; it demands the ability to critically evaluate AI capabilities, recognize their limitations, and integrate these technologies responsibly into organizational processes (Brynjolfsson & McAfee, 2014; Davenport & Ronanki, 2018).

This interactive workbook complements the official Brightspace learning materials by emphasizing experiential learning through practical demonstrations, guided experimentation, and real-world business applications. Rather than repeating the conceptual discussions provided in the course readings, the notebook enables students to explore how generative AI systems function, experiment with prompt engineering techniques, examine tokenization, and analyze authentic organizational documents using modern AI tools. Throughout the course, students will work with the Microsoft Enterprise Knowledge Repository (MEKR)-a curated collection of publicly available corporate documents that serves as the primary case study for applying generative AI in executive decision-making and organizational leadership.

By the end of this workshop, students will have developed a foundational understanding of the principles underlying generative AI and Large Language Models while gaining practical experience applying these technologies to realistic business scenarios. This foundation prepares students for progressively more advanced topics, including prompt engineering, retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), multimodal AI systems, and AI-assisted organizational decision support in subsequent workshops.

2. Core Concepts and Terminologies¶

2.1 Artificial Intelligence (AI)¶

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is a branch of computer science concerned with designing systems capable of performing tasks that normally require human intelligence, including reasoning, learning, problem solving, perception, language understanding, and decision making (Russell & Norvig, 2021). Modern AI encompasses a wide range of approaches, including rule-based systems, machine learning, deep learning, reinforcement learning, and generative AI.

Why It Matters¶

Artificial intelligence is transforming virtually every industry by enabling organizations to automate processes, improve decision-making, discover patterns in large datasets, and augment human capabilities. As AI continues to evolve, leaders must understand not only what AI can accomplish but also its limitations, ethical implications, and strategic value.

Example¶

Examples of AI applications include:

  • Recommendation systems (Netflix, Amazon)
  • Fraud detection in banking
  • Medical image diagnosis
  • Autonomous vehicles
  • Intelligent virtual assistants such as Microsoft Copilot and ChatGPT

Key Takeaway

Artificial Intelligence is the broader discipline that encompasses machine learning, deep learning, and generative AI technologies.

2.2 Generative Artificial Intelligence¶

Generative Artificial Intelligence (Generative AI) is a specialized branch of artificial intelligence that focuses on creating new content by learning patterns and representations from existing data. Unlike traditional AI systems that primarily classify, predict, or detect patterns, generative AI models can produce original outputs such as text, images, audio, video, computer code, and synthetic data that closely resemble human-created content (Goodfellow et al., 2016). Recent advancements in deep learning, particularly transformer-based architectures, have significantly enhanced the ability of generative AI systems to generate coherent, contextually relevant, and high-quality content across multiple domains (Vaswani et al., 2017).

Generative AI models are typically trained on massive datasets using self-supervised learning techniques, enabling them to capture complex linguistic, visual, and semantic relationships without requiring manually labeled data. These models have become foundational technologies for applications such as conversational AI, document summarization, software development, content creation, scientific research, and business decision support.

Why It Matters¶

Generative AI is fundamentally changing how organizations create, process, and leverage information. Businesses increasingly employ generative AI to automate repetitive knowledge-intensive tasks, enhance employee productivity, support strategic decision-making, improve customer engagement, accelerate software development, and generate insights from large volumes of unstructured data. Consequently, understanding the capabilities and limitations of generative AI has become an essential competency for modern business leaders, data professionals, and AI practitioners.

Example¶

Examples of Generative AI applications include:

  • ChatGPT for conversational assistance and content generation
  • Microsoft Copilot for workplace productivity
  • GitHub Copilot for AI-assisted software development
  • DALL·E for image generation
  • Sora for AI-generated video creation
  • NotebookLM for document understanding and knowledge synthesis

Key Takeaway

Generative Artificial Intelligence extends traditional AI by creating new content rather than simply analyzing existing data, enabling organizations to automate knowledge work and support intelligent decision-making across diverse business functions.

2.3 Large Language Models (LLMs)¶

Large Language Models (LLMs) are a class of deep learning models specifically designed to understand, generate, and manipulate human language. Built upon transformer architectures, LLMs are trained on massive corpora of text using self-supervised learning techniques that enable them to predict the next token in a sequence. Through this training process, LLMs learn complex linguistic structures, semantic relationships, contextual dependencies, and patterns of human language without requiring manually labeled datasets (Brown et al., 2020; Devlin et al., 2019; Vaswani et al., 2017).

Modern LLMs contain billions or even trillions of parameters that capture statistical representations of language. Rather than memorizing information, these models learn mathematical representations that allow them to generate coherent, contextually appropriate, and often highly informative responses. Consequently, LLMs have become the foundation for numerous generative AI applications, including conversational assistants, intelligent search, document summarization, software development, translation, question answering, and knowledge management.

Why It Matters¶

Large Language Models are rapidly transforming knowledge-intensive industries by augmenting human expertise rather than merely automating repetitive tasks. Organizations increasingly leverage LLMs to support executive decision-making, improve customer service, generate business reports, analyze large document repositories, assist software developers, and facilitate organizational learning. As these models become integral components of enterprise information systems, understanding their capabilities, limitations, and responsible use is an essential competency for business leaders, data scientists, and AI practitioners.

Example¶

Examples of widely used Large Language Models include:

  • GPT-4 and GPT-4o (OpenAI)
  • Claude (Anthropic)
  • Gemini (Google)
  • Llama (Meta)
  • Mistral
  • DeepSeek

Although these models differ in architecture, training methodology, and deployment strategies, they all rely on transformer-based neural networks to process natural language and generate human-like responses.

Key Takeaway

Large Language Models form the technological foundation of modern generative AI systems, enabling organizations to analyze information, generate content, support decision-making, and interact with users through natural language.

2.4 Transformer Architecture¶

The Transformer architecture is the foundational deep learning framework that powers modern Large Language Models (LLMs). Introduced by Vaswani et al. (2017), the Transformer fundamentally changed natural language processing by replacing sequential processing methods, such as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, with a mechanism known as self-attention. This innovation enables models to process entire sequences of text simultaneously while learning the contextual relationships between words regardless of their position within a sentence.

Unlike earlier neural network architectures that processed language one word at a time, Transformer models analyze all tokens in parallel. This parallel processing significantly improves computational efficiency, enables training on massive datasets, and allows the model to capture long-range dependencies within text. These capabilities have made Transformer architectures the foundation of virtually all modern generative AI systems, including GPT, Claude, Gemini, Llama, and many other state-of-the-art language models.

A Transformer model consists of several interconnected components that work together to process language effectively. First, text is converted into numerical representations known as embeddings, allowing the model to represent semantic meaning mathematically. Positional encoding is then added so the model understands the order of words within a sequence. The self-attention mechanism determines which words or phrases are most relevant to one another, while multiple stacked neural network layers progressively learn increasingly complex linguistic patterns. Finally, the model predicts the most probable next token based on the context of the preceding sequence.

Why It Matters¶

The Transformer architecture represents one of the most influential breakthroughs in modern artificial intelligence. Its ability to efficiently process massive amounts of textual information has enabled the development of highly capable Large Language Models that support applications such as conversational AI, document summarization, translation, code generation, knowledge retrieval, and intelligent decision support. Consequently, understanding the basic principles of the Transformer architecture provides an essential conceptual foundation for understanding how contemporary generative AI systems function.

Example¶

The simplified workflow of a Transformer-based Large Language Model can be summarized as follows:

User Prompt
      │
      ▼
Tokenization
      │
      ▼
Word Embeddings
      │
      ▼
Positional Encoding
      │
      ▼
Self-Attention Mechanism
      │
      ▼
Transformer Layers
      │
      ▼
Next Token Prediction
      │
      ▼
Generated Response

Key Takeaway

The Transformer architecture is the computational foundation of modern Large Language Models, enabling efficient language understanding and generation through self-attention, parallel processing, and contextual learning.

2.5 Prompt Engineering¶

Prompt engineering is the process of designing, refining, and optimizing instructions provided to generative AI systems in order to produce accurate, relevant, and contextually appropriate responses. A prompt serves as the primary interface between a user and a Large Language Model (LLM), guiding the model's reasoning, content generation, and decision-making processes. The quality, specificity, and structure of a prompt significantly influence the quality of the generated output. Consequently, effective prompt engineering has emerged as a fundamental skill for professionals who utilize generative AI in research, business, education, healthcare, software development, and numerous other domains (White et al., 2023).

Modern prompt engineering extends beyond simply asking questions. Well-designed prompts typically provide contextual information, define the desired role or perspective, specify the expected output format, establish constraints, and clearly communicate the objective of the task. By reducing ambiguity and providing sufficient context, users can substantially improve the accuracy, consistency, and usefulness of AI-generated responses. Prompt engineering continues to evolve alongside advances in generative AI. Contemporary techniques include zero-shot prompting, few-shot prompting, chain-of-thought prompting, role prompting, and retrieval-augmented prompting, each designed to improve model performance for specific tasks and problem domains.

Why It Matters¶

Prompt engineering enables organizations to maximize the value of generative AI systems while minimizing errors, ambiguity, and inconsistent outputs. Business professionals increasingly rely on effective prompting to automate document generation, summarize complex reports, support strategic decision-making, create marketing content, generate software code, analyze customer feedback, and interact with enterprise knowledge repositories. As generative AI becomes integrated into organizational workflows, prompt engineering is emerging as a critical competency for leaders, analysts, and AI practitioners.

Example¶

Consider the following prompts:

Basic Prompt

Summarize Microsoft's annual report.

Improved Prompt

Assume the role of a Chief Financial Officer (CFO). Summarize Microsoft's 2025 Annual Report in approximately 250 words, highlighting the company's financial performance, major business risks, strategic investments in artificial intelligence, and future growth opportunities. Present the summary using concise executive-level language.

The second prompt provides significantly more context, defines the user's role, specifies the expected output, and clearly identifies the information to be included. As a result, the generated response is typically more focused, informative, and aligned with organizational decision-making needs.

Key Takeaway

Prompt engineering is both an art and a scientific process that enables users to communicate effectively with Large Language Models, transforming general requests into precise instructions that produce reliable, context-aware, and actionable AI-generated outputs.

2.6 Tokenization¶

Tokenization is the process of breaking text into smaller units, known as tokens, that can be processed by a Large Language Model (LLM). Tokens are the fundamental building blocks of language models and may represent complete words, subwords, punctuation marks, or even individual characters, depending on the tokenization algorithm employed. Before a prompt can be interpreted by an LLM, the input text must first be converted into tokens and subsequently transformed into numerical representations that the neural network can process (Jurafsky & Martin, 2025).

Modern LLMs typically employ subword tokenization techniques, such as Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) or SentencePiece, to efficiently represent language while balancing vocabulary size and computational efficiency. This approach enables models to understand common words as well as unfamiliar terms, abbreviations, and domain-specific vocabulary by decomposing them into smaller, meaningful units.

The number of tokens contained within a prompt directly influences computational cost, response length, processing time, and the amount of contextual information the model can retain. Consequently, understanding tokenization enables users to design more efficient prompts, optimize interactions with LLMs, and better interpret model limitations.

Why It Matters¶

Tokenization is a fundamental process underlying every interaction with a Large Language Model. Every prompt submitted to systems such as ChatGPT, Microsoft Copilot, Claude, or Gemini is first converted into tokens before the model performs language understanding or text generation. Because most commercial LLMs impose limits on the number of tokens that can be processed in a single conversation, understanding tokenization allows users to structure prompts more effectively, manage context efficiently, and reduce computational costs.

Example¶

Consider the following sentence:

Microsoft is investing heavily in Generative AI technologies.

A tokenizer may divide the sentence into tokens similar to the following:

Token Number Token
1 Microsoft
2 is
3 investing
4 heavily
5 in
6 Generative
7 AI
8 technologies
9 .

Although this example appears straightforward, modern tokenizers frequently divide uncommon words into multiple subword tokens to improve efficiency and enable the model to process previously unseen vocabulary.

Key Takeaway

Tokenization is the process of converting human language into machine-readable tokens. Every prompt and every AI-generated response begins with tokenization, making it one of the most fundamental processes underlying Large Language Models.

2.7 Context Window¶

The context window refers to the maximum amount of information that a Large Language Model (LLM) can process and retain during a single interaction. This information includes the user's prompt, previous conversation history, uploaded documents, system instructions, and the model's generated responses. The context window is measured in tokens, rather than words or characters, making tokenization a critical factor in determining how much information can be processed at one time (Bommasani et al., 2021).

As users interact with an LLM, every prompt and response contributes to the available context. When the total number of tokens exceeds the model's maximum context window, earlier portions of the conversation may be truncated or discarded to accommodate new information. Consequently, the model's ability to maintain continuity, recall previous details, and generate contextually consistent responses depends directly on the available context window. Modern LLMs vary considerably in their context window capacities. While early language models were limited to processing only a few thousand tokens, contemporary models can process hundreds of thousands or even millions of tokens, enabling the analysis of lengthy reports, research papers, legal contracts, software repositories, and enterprise knowledge bases.

Why It Matters¶

Understanding the context window is essential when working with enterprise documents and knowledge-intensive applications. Business professionals frequently interact with lengthy annual reports, policy documents, financial statements, and technical documentation that may exceed the processing limits of an AI model. Knowledge of context windows enables users to structure prompts more effectively, divide large documents into manageable sections, and design AI workflows that maximize response quality while minimizing information loss.

Example¶

Suppose a business analyst uploads Microsoft's Annual Report to an AI assistant and asks:

"Summarize the company's financial performance, AI strategy, sustainability initiatives, risk factors, and executive outlook."

If the document exceeds the model's available context window, only a portion of the report may be processed during a single interaction. The analyst may therefore divide the report into sections or employ Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) techniques to selectively retrieve the most relevant information before generating a response.

Key Takeaway

The context window defines how much information a Large Language Model can consider during a conversation. Understanding this limitation enables users to design more effective prompts, manage large documents efficiently, and improve the quality and consistency of AI-generated responses.

2.8 Multimodal Artificial Intelligence¶

Multimodal Artificial Intelligence (Multimodal AI) refers to AI systems capable of processing, integrating, and generating information from multiple forms of data, including text, images, audio, video, documents, and structured data. Unlike traditional AI systems that operate on a single data modality, multimodal models combine information from diverse sources to develop a richer contextual understanding of complex tasks. This capability enables AI systems to reason across different types of information, thereby improving the accuracy, flexibility, and usefulness of their responses (Bommasani et al., 2021).

Recent advances in transformer architectures have significantly accelerated the development of multimodal AI systems. Modern foundation models can simultaneously analyze written documents, interpret images, recognize speech, generate computer code, and respond to complex user queries using multiple input formats. As a result, multimodal AI is expanding the practical applications of generative AI across business, healthcare, education, engineering, finance, scientific research, and numerous other disciplines. Rather than viewing text, images, audio, and video as independent information sources, multimodal AI integrates these modalities into a unified representation, allowing models to understand relationships that would be difficult to identify using a single data type. This capability represents a major advancement toward more intelligent, context-aware AI systems that more closely resemble human communication and reasoning.

Why It Matters¶

Organizations increasingly generate and manage information in multiple formats, including reports, presentations, spreadsheets, images, emails, videos, and voice recordings. Multimodal AI enables organizations to analyze these diverse information sources simultaneously, improving decision-making, knowledge discovery, customer engagement, and operational efficiency. Business leaders who understand multimodal AI will be better positioned to leverage next-generation AI technologies that support comprehensive enterprise intelligence and digital transformation.

Example¶

Consider an executive reviewing Microsoft's Annual Report together with accompanying charts, financial statements, product images, and a recorded earnings call.

A multimodal AI system can:

  • Summarize the written report.
  • Interpret financial charts and graphs.
  • Analyze product images and visual presentations.
  • Transcribe and summarize executive speeches.
  • Integrate insights from all these sources into a single executive briefing.

This ability to reason across multiple forms of information significantly enhances organizational decision support compared to analyzing each source independently.

Key Takeaway

Multimodal Artificial Intelligence extends the capabilities of Large Language Models by enabling AI systems to understand and generate information across multiple data modalities, creating richer, more context-aware interactions that support increasingly sophisticated business and organizational applications.

3.7 Tokenization Demonstration¶

Although humans read documents as words, sentences, and paragraphs, Large Language Models process text as tokens. A token is the basic unit of text that an LLM uses during training and inference. Depending on the tokenizer, a token may represent an entire word, part of a word, punctuation, or even whitespace.

Tokenization is the first computational step performed before a prompt reaches the transformer architecture. Every prompt and every generated response is converted into a sequence of tokens that the model can process numerically.

In this demonstration, we illustrate the concept of tokenization using a simple whitespace tokenizer. Although modern LLMs employ more sophisticated tokenization algorithms, this example provides an intuitive understanding of how textual information is transformed into smaller computational units.

In [15]:

Although the exact number of tokens produced by an LLM tokenizer differs from this simplified example, the underlying concept remains the same: textual information must first be converted into a sequence of tokens before it can be processed by the transformer architecture. Understanding tokenization is essential because model context windows, computational cost, inference speed, and API pricing are typically measured in tokens rather than words or pages.

The knowledge and practical skills developed in this workshop provide the conceptual foundation for the remaining workshops, where students will progressively design, evaluate, and implement more sophisticated Large Language Model applications using authentic enterprise data and real-world business scenarios.
